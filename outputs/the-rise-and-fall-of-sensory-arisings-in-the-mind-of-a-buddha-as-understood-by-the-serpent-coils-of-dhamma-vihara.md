Homage to the Blessed One, the Worthy One, the Fully Enlightened. Today we take up a delicate and very practical topic: the rise and fall of sensory arisings in the mind of a Buddha, as understood through the serpent coils of Dhamma Vihara. This is not an invitation to speculate about what we cannot verify, nor to become anxious about whether “a Buddha had sensory contact.” Rather, it is a guided way to see clearly how contact happens at the sense doors, how feeling and perception arise from it, and how the wise knowing sees both the rising and the falling without grasping.

When the Dhamma speaks about “sensory arisings,” it is speaking about events. Not a thing. Not a self. Sensory arisings are the mind’s experience of sights, sounds, smells, tastes, touches, and mental objects—arising because conditions meet: sense faculty, object, and attention. In the Buddha’s life, sensory contact was not absent. What was absent was the clinging that turns contact into “me” and “mine,” and turns experience into suffering. So if you ever wonder, “Did the Buddha have sense experience?” the wise answer is: yes, there is contact and there is knowing; but there is no grasping. The point of the teaching is non-identification, not sensory suppression.

Let us begin with a reflection. Have you noticed how quickly the mind makes something personal out of what is happening? A sound occurs, and within a breath the mind says, “That is unpleasant,” or “That is mine,” or “I must fix it,” or “I cannot stand it.” The Dhamma trains us to see that this second step—grasping, aversion, and the sense of ownership—is not inevitable. There is a process. And when we see the process clearly, liberation becomes possible.

Now, the serpent coils of Dhamma Vihara give us a helpful metaphor. Imagine a serpent coiled, steady and concentrated. The coils represent conditions and continuity: attention that gathers itself around experience, staying close, not drifting away. In practice, mindfulness winds itself around sensory events in a continuous, precise way. It does not strangle experience; it embraces it with clarity. Then, when insight matures, the coil unwinds. The mind stops tightening around “what should happen” and begins to see things as they are: arising, changing, and ceasing. In that unwinding, the mind becomes liberated. So the serpent coil is both the discipline—continuous awareness—and the wisdom—seeing impermanence and non-grasping.

To understand this, we must map the sense-door process in a simple step-by-step way. The Buddha’s teaching does not ask us to guess. It asks us to observe.

First, the sense doors: eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and also mind. Each has its own role. The eye is the faculty; the visible form is the object; and attention brings them into contact. The same pattern repeats at the ear with sound, at the nose with smell, and so on. Notice the word “contact.” In Pali, phassa, contact is the meeting point. It is not merely “an object exists.” It is not merely “awareness is present.” Contact is the moment when the conditions align and experience becomes present to mind.

Second, phassa leads to sensory arisings that are noticed as happening. When contact is present, the mind experiences a “felt knowing” of that object. This is where many meditators get confused. They think that because they are meditating, they must block contact or push sensation away. But the Dhamma does not train us to block. It trains us to know. The serpent coil practice is not hiding; it is attending. So at the sense door, we let the experience be there, and we know it as experience.

Third, from contact, feeling arises. Feeling in the Dhamma is vedanā: pleasant, unpleasant, or neither-pleasant-nor-unpleasant. Feeling is immediate and subtle. It is the tone of experience. When you hear a sound, there may be pleasantness; when you touch something sharp, there may be unpleasantness; when you hear a neutral sound or notice a neutral sensation, there may be a neutral tone. Feeling is not yet craving. Feeling is the raw tone that appears with contact.

Fourth, feeling becomes perception and tendency. Perception in Pali is saññā: recognition, labeling, the mind’s “making meaning.” The mind may recognize “this is pain,” or “this is danger,” or “this is beautiful,” and then the tendencies follow—craving, aversion, or delusion. This is where suffering grows. Not because feeling is evil, but because the mind reacts: it grabs at what it likes and pushes away what it dislikes, and then it weaves a story: “I am being attacked,” “I deserve better,” “I am safe,” “I am not safe,” “I must control.”

Let us speak gently but clearly about “rise and fall.” “Rise” is the process of contact becoming feeling, perception, and then the mind’s reaction. “Fall” is when these processes fade as conditions change. The Buddha’s mind is described as knowing yathābhūtañāṇa—knowledge of things as they truly are. In that knowledge, rise is seen as rise; fall is seen as fall. There is no need to add “this is me” or “this will last” or “this must be controlled.” The knowing is direct.

Now we can approach the specific question many people carry: “Did the Buddha have sensory arisings?” If we misunderstand, we may swing to extremes. One extreme is to assume the Buddha had no sensory contact, as if the senses were absent. Another extreme is to assume that the Buddha’s mind was like ours—reactive and grasping. Both are errors. The Buddha’s senses function, sensory contact happens, and feeling and perception arise. The difference is the Buddha’s mode of knowing. He does not identify with the arisings. He does not take them as a self. He does not allow the arisings to become a basis for clinging.

So we can say this: sensory arisings may occur, but clinging does not take hold. When contact happens, the Buddha knows: “Contact is there.” When feeling arises, he knows: “Feeling arises.” When perception and tendency arise, he knows: “Perception is happening.” And when conditions change, he knows: “This is ceasing.” This is how the Buddha’s mind is “non-identifying.” It is not sensory absence. It is non-grasping.

At this point, it helps to introduce a practical phrase from dependent origination and mindfulness training: “When this is, that is.” Conditions bring experience. But the Buddha also shows the cessation component: “When this ceases, that ceases.” This is crucial for understanding nirodha. In daily practice, many people only watch the rise and never watch the fall. They see irritation begin, but they do not notice how irritation can dissolve when attention changes and conditions shift. The Dhamma invites you to watch the ending too. That ending is part of the path.

Now let us return to the serpent coils. When the coil is tight—when attention is caught in liking or disliking—there is grasping and suffering. When mindfulness becomes steady, the coil becomes a protective embrace: the mind stays with the sense-door experience without tightening. Then insight unwinds the coil further: it becomes clear that what arises is conditioned, not a self, and what falls is also conditioned, not a personal loss. In that clarity, clinging loses its fuel.

Let us do a guided mapping, step by step, using one sense door. You can choose whatever is easiest right now. If there is sound around you, use hearing. If there is pressure in your body, use touch. If there is a visible object near you, use seeing.

First step: sense-door. Ask quietly, “Which door is active?” “Is it hearing now, or is it seeing, or is it touch?” Don’t argue with yourself. Just observe what is most prominent.

Second step: contact. Notice the meeting. In hearing, it is the mind meeting sound. In touch, it is the mind meeting pressure or warmth. Let the “meeting” be known without turning it into a story.

Third step: feeling tone. Ask, “Is it pleasant, unpleasant, or neutral?” If you cannot tell, be honest: “There is a tone, but I’m not sure.” Even uncertainty can be known as experience.

Fourth step: perception and labeling. Let a gentle label arise: “hearing,” “pain,” “thinking,” “tightness,” “warmth,” “pleasant,” “unpleasant.” But do not grab the label as truth; treat it as a signpost. The serpent coil practice is precise, not rigid.

Fifth step: observe rise and fall. Now watch what happens next. Does the feeling tone intensify? Does it soften? Does it shift into neutral? Does the perception change? Does the mind react? If reaction arises—craving or aversion—know it as reaction, too. This is important: the Buddha’s mode is not “never having reaction.” The Buddha’s mode is knowing reaction without being captured by it.

When you see this clearly, you begin to understand the Buddha’s “rise and fall” in your own experience. The Buddha is not teaching you a mythic psychology. He is teaching you the same mechanics you can observe right now.

Now, how does this connect to the Buddha’s mode “knowing without grasping”? In the suttas, the wise training emphasizes clear comprehension—sampajañña—knowing what is happening in the body and mind while doing it. This is not vague awareness. It is knowing with clarity. So when you notice “this arises,” you also notice “this is conditioned.” And when you notice “this falls,” you notice “this is ceasing.” That is the cessation component being tasted in real time.

There is also a moral dimension here, because the precepts are not just rules; they are protective pathways for mindfulness. Let’s apply the precepts to sensory arisings in daily life.

When sensual impulses arise—images, sounds, touches that can stir desire—the precept of refraining from sexual misconduct begins as mindfulness at the sense door. You do not need to wait until you are doing something harmful. You can notice the arousing contact, notice the pleasant feeling tone, and notice the impulse to grasp. Then you choose a wise response: you allow the impulse to be seen and you do not follow it into speech or action. Desire is not automatically wrong; grasping is what leads to suffering and harm.

Similarly, the precept of refraining from killing is supported by how we respond to aversion and fear. When you see something you dislike, the mind can become hostile. But if you practice knowing sensory contact without feeding aversion, you reduce the chain that can lead to harm. You see aversion as a feeling tone and a perception process, and you let it pass without tightening into cruelty.

The precept of refraining from stealing can be supported by noticing greed arising from contact. When you see something valuable, contact arises, feeling tone arises, and perception may say, “Mine.” If you watch that process early, you can see greed before it becomes action. You can then choose honesty, contentment, and restraint.

The precept of refraining from lying is also connected. When sensory experience is not clearly seen, the mind may distort it to protect itself. But when mindfulness is precise, you speak from direct knowledge. You don’t need to embellish. You don’t need to deny what you know.

The precept of refraining from intoxication supports clarity of observation. If the mind is clouded, sensory arisings become chaotic and the serpent coil cannot hold steady. Clarity makes it possible to see rise and fall accurately.

And right speech and right conduct—this is the heart of practical ethics—are cultivated by non-harmful, non-reactive awareness. When a reactive impulse arises, you pause. You know. You allow the impulse to be experienced without acting it out. That is right conduct in motion.

Now, let me address common misunderstandings, because they often block progress. Some meditators think they must block senses to be “Buddha-like.” But the Dhamma does not teach blocking. It teaches seeing with mindfulness and letting go. If you block, you may create tension, and the mind becomes less honest. Others think, “The Buddha had no sensory arisings, so I should have none.” That creates fear and suppresses natural experience. Yet the Buddha’s teaching is about non-identification. Still others treat awareness as a fixed entity—“my awareness is the same forever.” But the Dhamma trains you to see awareness as part of a process: attention meets contact, and knowing arises and changes. If awareness becomes a “thing,” clinging returns. The serpent coil metaphor helps here: awareness is like a winding and unwinding, not a permanent statue.

Now, let us practice for a few moments together, slowly and gently. Choose one sense door. If you are sitting, perhaps touch is easiest. Notice the body at the point where sensation is most clear—hands, feet, or breath contact. Then, in your mind, do five quiet steps, repeating as needed.

First, “touch door.” Second, “contact is present.” Third, “feeling tone: pleasant, unpleasant, or neutral.” Fourth, “perception: naming the experience.” Fifth, “rise and fall: notice whether it changes, intensifies, softens, or ceases.” If you get lost in thoughts, do not scold yourself. Simply return to the sense door and begin again. This is how the serpent coil is maintained: continuous, not perfect.

As you do this, you may notice something important: when you stop grasping, the mind becomes calmer. Sometimes the sensation remains, but the suffering reduces. That is the path working. The “fall” is not always the disappearance of the sensation immediately; it is the falling away of clinging and the falling away of the reactive story. Eventually, with deeper insight, even the remaining process becomes seen as impermanent and non-self.

Now we come to the heart of the teaching: anicca and nirodha. Impermanence is not only an intellectual concept; it is visible at the sense door. Watch the sensation. Watch the feeling tone. Watch the perception and the reaction. You will see change. When conditions shift, the experience ends or transforms. That ending is nirodha—cessation. And the Buddha’s knowing includes both: “When it arises, I know it arises. When it ceases, I know it ceases.” That is how cessation becomes part of practice, not only a distant doctrine.

So, if you want one simple instruction to carry into daily life, let it be this: at the moment of contact—when you see, hear, smell, taste, or touch—pause and register the process. Don’t rush into story. Don’t rush into grasping or aversion. Just know: “contact,” “feeling tone,” “perception,” and “this is changing.” Then respond with wisdom aligned with the precepts. That is how the serpent coils of Dhamma Vihara become liberating, not merely poetic.
